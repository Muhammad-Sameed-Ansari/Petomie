#Auditory Structures#

##Location##
The auditory system is located in the head, extending from the outer ear through the middle ear to the inner ear, which is encased in the temporal bone. 
It connects to the brain through the auditory nerve and works closely with the vestibular system to support both hearing and balance.

###Shape###
- Outer Ear: Includes the pinna (ear flap) and ear canal, which funnel sound waves inward.  
- Middle Ear: An air-filled chamber containing three small bones called the ossicles — the malleus (hammer), incus (anvil), and stapes (stirrup).  
- Inner Ear: Contains the cochlea (hearing organ) and vestibular apparatus (balance organs).  
- Auditory Nerve: Carries sound information from the cochlea to the brain.

##Function##
- The pinna collects sound waves and directs them through the ear canal to the tympanic membrane (eardrum).  
- The eardrum vibrates, moving the ossicles, which amplify and transmit sound to the cochlea.  
- In the cochlea, fluid vibrations stimulate tiny hair cells that convert sound energy into electrical impulses.  
- These signals travel along the auditory nerve to the brain, where they are interpreted as sound.  
- The system also contributes to balance through its connection with the vestibular structures.  

Horses can detect low and high-frequency sounds and move their large, mobile ears independently to locate sound sources quickly.
