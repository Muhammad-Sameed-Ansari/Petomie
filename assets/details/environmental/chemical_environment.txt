#Chemical Environment#

###What It Is###
The chemical environment refers to all chemical substances the animal is exposed to in its surroundings, whether through air, water, surfaces, soil, food, or direct application. This includes household chemicals, agricultural products, stable or kennel treatments, pest control substances, industrial pollutants, plastics, and residues. Chemical exposure can affect the physical body, nervous system, detox organs, immune function, hormones, and the energy field. Even low-level or indirect exposure can create stress when the animal’s detox capacity is exceeded.

###How It May Form###
Chemical environment stress may form through exposure to pesticides, herbicides, insecticides, cleaning agents, disinfectants, air fresheners, sprays, paints, solvents, treated wood, plastics, mold toxins, heavy metals, contaminated water, or polluted air. It may also form through regular use of flea and tick treatments, dewormers, fly sprays, stable disinfectants, or chemically treated equipment. Stress can arise from acute exposure or accumulate slowly through chronic, repeated contact with small amounts over time.

###Possible Effects###
Chemical environment stress may contribute to skin irritation, itching, respiratory issues, digestive imbalance, liver or kidney overload, immune suppression, hormonal disruption, fatigue, neurological sensitivity, behavioral changes, anxiety, restlessness, or shutdown. Symptoms may appear vague, shifting, or resistant to conventional treatment.

###Energetic & Nervous System Impact###
Chemical exposure can burden the detox pathways and place ongoing stress on the nervous system. It may interfere with cellular communication, energetic flow, grounding, and overall resilience. Chronic exposure can keep the body in a state of low-grade defense or overwhelm, reducing the animal’s ability to fully recover and regulate.
